# Fallen arch exercises

Personal (non-work) task: a leg-strength program addressing fallen arches
(adult-acquired flatfoot / overpronation) and the knee pain they drive through
the kinetic chain. The program is a **daily ~5-minute foot mini-routine** plus
a **~20-minute strength session 3×/week** — the realistic minimum effective
dose for a chronic pattern.

## Background — what's likely happening

The pattern is well recognized in podiatry and sports medicine: when the
medial arch collapses, the foot rolls inward (pronates) more than it should
during the stance phase of walking. The inward roll rotates the tibia
internally, pulling the knee into a slight valgus (inward) position. Over
thousands of steps a day this creates abnormal loading and tracking at the
kneecap.

Syndromes this commonly produces:

- **Patellofemoral pain syndrome** — pain around or behind the kneecap, often
  worse going down stairs
- **IT band syndrome** — outer knee/thigh pain
- **Medial knee strain** from the valgus loading
- **Compensatory stiffness** in the calves, tibialis posterior, and hip
  flexors, which work overtime trying to stabilize what the bones aren't
  doing efficiently

## Daily foot routine (~5 min, ideally barefoot)

The part most people skip because it feels too small to matter — it's
actually the highest-leverage work for arch reconstruction, but only if it's
daily. Can be done while brushing teeth, waiting for coffee, etc.; it does
not need to be a "session."

1. **Short foot exercise** — sit or stand with foot flat. Without curling the
   toes, draw the ball of the foot toward the heel, doming the arch. The
   muscles in the arch should be working, not the toes. Hold 5 s, relax.
   10 reps per foot. *The foundational intrinsic foot strengthener — if you
   only do one thing, do this.*
2. **Toe yoga** — press the big toe down while lifting the other four; then
   reverse (lift the big toe, press the other four down). 10 reps each
   direction, each foot. Awkward at first, gets easier in a week. Improves
   the toe control needed for proper push-off.
3. **Single-leg balance, barefoot** — 30 s per foot, twice. Progress when
   easy: close the eyes, or stand on a folded towel/pillow. Trains the
   proprioceptive system that's the foot's first line of defense against
   collapsing arches.

## Strength session (3×/week, ~20–25 min)

**Warm-up (2–3 min):** banded monster walks, mini-band around or just above
the knees — 10 steps right, left, forward, backward. Wakes up the glute
medius; if it doesn't fire in the warm-up it probably won't fire in the
working sets either.

**Main work — three rounds.** Do each exercise as a set, rest 30–60 s, move
to the next.

1. **Single-leg glute bridge** — 8–10 reps/side. Lie on back, one foot
   planted, other leg extended. Drive through the planted heel, lift hips,
   squeeze the glute at the top; keep the pelvis level, no tilting. Glute max
   more than medius, but transfers directly to push-off mechanics.
2. **Side-lying leg raises** — 12–15 reps/side. Bottom leg bent for
   stability, top leg straight. Lift with the heel slightly behind the hip
   line and toes pointing slightly down — this isolates glute medius and
   keeps the hip flexor from taking over (the most common mistake). The burn
   belongs in the side of the hip, not the front. Add a mini-band around the
   thighs once bodyweight is easy.
3. **Single-leg Romanian deadlift** — 6–8 reps/side, dumbbell in the opposite
   hand. Hinge at the hip, flat back, free leg extends behind for
   counterbalance, standing knee soft. Control the descent. Probably the
   single best exercise for hip stability, knee tracking, and foot/ankle
   proprioception in one movement; start light (or unweighted) until balance
   is solid.
4. **Reverse lunge** — 8 reps/side, holding dumbbells. Step *backward* and
   lower the back knee toward the floor — reverse lunges load the knee much
   less than forward lunges while working the same range of motion that
   troubles patellofemoral issues. The front knee should track over the
   second toe, not collapse inward (check in a mirror or by feel).
5. **Eccentric single-leg calf raise** — 8 reps/side, barefoot or in socks.
   Stand on a step with heels hanging off, rise on both feet, shift weight to
   one foot, lower slowly over 3 s. The slow eccentric builds Achilles tendon
   resilience — essential prep for gradually adopting lower-drop shoes.
6. **Side plank** — 20–30 s/side, building to 45–60 s over weeks. Knees-down
   version is fine initially. Hugely valuable for glute-medius transfer to
   standing/walking and for the trunk stability that prevents the
   pelvis-drop pattern.

## Progression

- **Weeks 1–2:** form, not load. Some of these — especially the single-leg
  RDL and the side-lying raise — are sneakily technical and easy to do wrong
  in ways that feel productive but train the wrong muscles. Film one set per
  exercise from the side and check.
- **Weeks 3–6:** add load — heavier dumbbells on RDLs and lunges, bigger band
  on the side-lying work, full side plank instead of the knee version.
- **Beyond:** Bulgarian split squats as the natural progression from reverse
  lunges; lateral step-downs from a low box for knee tracking specifically;
  wobble board / BOSU for balance; weighted standing single-leg calf raises.

**Skip for now:** heavy back squats and conventional deadlifts — not because
they're bad, but because with a weak glute medius the knees collapse inward
under heavy bilateral loading, reinforcing exactly the pattern being undone.
Return to them once single-leg work feels strong.
